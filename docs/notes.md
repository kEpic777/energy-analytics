1. What is SDAC? What problem does it solve?
SDAC (Single Day-Ahead Coupling) is the pan-European mechanism that links national wholesale electricity markets into a single day-ahead auction, run jointly by NEMOs (power exchanges) and TSOs (grid operators). The core problem it solves is the inefficient, fragmented allocation of cross-border transmission capacity: before coupling, capacity and electricity had to be traded separately, which wasted grid headroom and split liquidity. SDAC runs all national order books through a single algorithm (EUPHEMIA) that simultaneously finds clearing prices and allocates scarce cross-border capacity to the trades with the highest social welfare. The result is price convergence between bidding zones whenever capacity permits, deeper liquidity, and more efficient use of generation across Europe. As of 2025, SDAC covers over 95% of European electricity consumption.

2. What is SIDC and how does it differ from SDAC?
SIDC (Single Intraday Coupling) is the equivalent European mechanism for trading electricity on the day of delivery, up to one hour before real-time. Where SDAC is a once-a-day batch auction clearing all hours of the next day simultaneously, SIDC is a continuous market: bids and offers are matched instantly as they arrive via the XBID platform, with each matched trade potentially at a different price. SIDC was added on top of SDAC because day-ahead forecasts are imperfect — especially with variable renewables — and market participants need a way to re-balance their positions after the day-ahead gate closes. In short: SDAC sets the baseline positions the night before; SIDC lets participants adjust those positions in real time as conditions evolve. Since June 2024, SIDC also runs three intraday auctions (IDA1/2/3) alongside continuous trading to better price cross-border congestion in the intraday window.

3. What is BSP's role as NEMO in Slovenia?
BSP (Borzen's daughter company, BSP SouthPool) was designated as Slovenia's Nominated Electricity Market Operator (NEMO) by the Slovenian Energy Agency in December 2023, for an indefinite period. As NEMO, BSP is the entity legally responsible for running both the single day-ahead and intraday coupling within Slovenian territory under the EU CACM Regulation (2015/1222). In practice this means BSP operates the Slovenian day-ahead auction and connects it into SDAC, and similarly plugs into the XBID system for SIDC intraday continuous trading. BSP is also an active participant in pan-European NEMO governance bodies, contributing to algorithm methodology, testing, and working groups that develop the shared rules all NEMOs must follow.

4. What is the Balance Scheme? Who must be a member?
The Balance Scheme is the hierarchical framework that organises the Slovenian electricity market and governs how imbalances are tracked and settled. Any legal or natural person who wants to actively operate in the Slovenian electricity market must become a member — this includes traders, suppliers, the TSO (ELES), and the energy exchange (BSP). Members join either by signing a Balancing Agreement directly with the Market Operator (Borzen), or by signing a Compensation Agreement with an existing member. Within the scheme, participants are organised into Balance Groups, each headed by a Balance Responsible Party; Borzen, as Market Operator, sits at the top of the hierarchy and is responsible for ensuring balancing energy reaches those groups. Membership is also a prerequisite for recording closed contracts and operational forecasts with Borzen.

5. What is a Balance Responsible Party (BRP)?
A BRP is the entity within a Balance Group that takes legal and financial responsibility for keeping that group's aggregate electricity position balanced — meaning that the total of scheduled generation, consumption, and trades matches actual metered flows as closely as possible. Before entering the Balance Scheme, the BRP must sign a Balancing Agreement with Borzen on behalf of its group, and it is required to submit operational forecasts and report all closed contracts to the Market Operator. When actual delivery deviates from the schedule, the BRP incurs an imbalance charge set by Borzen and physically resolved by ELES; this financial exposure is the incentive for BRPs to trade actively in day-ahead and intraday markets to keep their position square. In Slovenia, any entity wishing to become a BRP must apply to Borzen and demonstrate financial solvency and other eligibility conditions.

6. What is the Platform for Balancing Energy?
The Platform for Balancing Energy is an organised, technology-supported system run by Borzen (the Market Operator) for collecting and matching bids to buy and sell balancing energy, with the goal of settling electricity system imbalances in a transparent and economically efficient way. In practice, ELES (the TSO) uses the platform to buy energy when the system is short and sell energy when it is long, with all trades settled at market prices. Trading on the platform runs alongside intraday trading and continues until half an hour after intraday closes — right up to the start of the delivery period. Any company that is a member of the Balance Scheme and has signed up for trading access (via BSP SouthPool) can participate.

7. What are aFRR, mFRR, and RR?
aFRR (automatic Frequency Restoration Reserve): The secondary reserve layer, activated automatically by the TSO within 30 seconds to 5 minutes of a frequency deviation, used to restore grid frequency to 50 Hz and gradually relieve the faster primary reserve (FCR).
mFRR (manual Frequency Restoration Reserve): The tertiary reserve, activated manually by grid operators within around 12.5 minutes; it supports or replaces aFRR when imbalances persist, and can involve ramping up power plants, adjusting cross-border flows, or demand response.
RR (Replacement Reserve): The slowest layer, activated within 30 minutes to several hours; its job is not to fix frequency directly but to replenish the faster reserves (aFRR, mFRR) so they are available again for the next event — essentially the strategic backstop.

8. What's the difference between 15-minute and hourly products?
On Borzen's balancing platform, hourly products cover a full 60-minute delivery window, while 15-minute products cover individual quarter-hour intervals. The shift toward 15-minute products matters because the accounting interval for imbalance settlement in Slovenia is already 15 minutes — so finer-grained products allow BRPs and ELES to correct imbalances at the same resolution at which they are measured and charged. Hourly products are coarser and cheaper to administer but can leave significant within-hour imbalances unaddressed, which becomes more of a problem as solar and wind generation fluctuate on sub-hourly timescales.

9. Why does balancing run 24/7?
Electricity supply and demand must be matched in real time at all hours — the physical grid does not pause overnight or on weekends. Frequency deviations can occur at any hour (a sudden generator trip, an unexpected load surge, a wind forecast error), and ELES as TSO must always have reserves available to correct them. The balancing platform therefore operates around the clock, seven days a week, with products available for trading at most one day in advance and up to the start of actual supply.

10. What is imbalance settlement?
Imbalance settlement is the after-the-fact financial reckoning for each Balance Group: Borzen compares the group's actual metered generation and consumption against its market plan (built from all recorded closed contracts) for every 15-minute accounting interval, calculates the volume of imbalance, and attaches a price to it. Settlement runs twice: a first pass in the month following the accounting month (M+1), which triggers financial payment, and a second corrective pass in M+2 if meter data was revised or disputed. The imbalance price is set by Borzen and is intentionally punitive — BRPs pay more for being short and receive less for being long than they would have in the market — which creates a strong incentive to trade actively in day-ahead and intraday markets rather than letting imbalances accumulate.
